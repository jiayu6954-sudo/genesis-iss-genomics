Dear Editor-in-Chief,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript, entitled **"Coordinated loss of CRISPR-Cas systems and insertion sequences in Bacillales from the International Space Station: evidence for defence system streamlining in a closed habitable environment"**, for consideration as a **Short Communication** in *npj Microgravity*.

**Scientific context and significance**

The International Space Station (ISS) microbiome has been characterised at the taxonomic level by landmark studies (e.g., Checinska Sielaff *et al.*, *Microbiome* 2019), yet the *genomic functional consequences* of sustained ISS residence remain poorly understood. Our study addresses this gap by performing the first species-stratified comparative genomics of ISS and ground Bacillales, systematically asking whether defence-associated genomic elements—CRISPR-Cas systems and insertion sequences (IS elements)—undergo directional reduction under closed-environment selection.

**Key findings**

Analysing 85 Bacillales genomes (30 ISS isolates, PRJNA637984; 55 ground controls) across four species, and applying rigorous species-stratification to control for natural interspecies variation, we demonstrate:

1. *Paenibacillus polymyxa* ISS isolates show complete CRISPR-Cas absence (0/9, 0%) compared with 53.3% prevalence in ground strains (Fisher's exact *p* = 0.0095, Benjamini–Hochberg FDR *q* = 0.025)—an ISS-specific signal absent from the other three species.

2. IS element density is significantly reduced in ISS *Bacillus thuringiensis* (0.115-fold; Cliff's δ = −1.0, BH *q* = 0.00015) and *P. polymyxa* (0.312-fold; δ = −1.0, BH *q* = 0.00023), with complete rank-order separation in both species.

3. Within *P. polymyxa*, CRISPR-absent strains harbour significantly lower IS densities than CRISPR-carrying strains (*p* = 0.003, δ = −0.75), indicating co-loss of the two defence compartments—a pattern inconsistent with simple CRISPR decay but consistent with coordinated defence investment reduction.

4. These findings are independently corroborated by metagenomics (NASA GeneLab GLDS-224): ISS debris samples show 50.3% lower CRISPR-associated KO density than ground controls (0.668 vs. 1.328 per 1,000 KO).

We propose that the ISS—as a physically sealed, phage-poor, transfer-limited environment—drives a form of coordinated genomic streamlining analogous to reductive evolution in host-restricted bacteria. Seven pre-specified methodological validation tests (assembly quality confound, annotation pipeline uniformity, keyword sensitivity, statistical power, multiple testing correction, bootstrap confidence intervals, and independent CRISPR validation) support the robustness of these findings.

**Fit to *npj Microgravity***

This work aligns directly with the journal's mission to publish original research on the effects of microgravity and space environments on biological systems. Recent publications in *npj Microgravity* have examined microgravity effects on bacterial physiology (Zea *et al.* 2016, 2:15029) and growth dynamics (Santomartino *et al.* 2020, 6:27); our study extends this literature to the genomic adaptation level, providing mechanistic insight into how the ISS environment shapes resident microbial genomes over evolutionary timescales. The concise Short Communication format is appropriate given the focused scope of our findings.
